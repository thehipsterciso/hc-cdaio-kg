# Stage 2: Business Model Economics


## Stage Orientation

> **Stage 2 Orientation - Business Model & Revenue Engine Reality**
> Status: `APPROVED`


### Stage 2 Intent


**Primary Purpose:** Determine how the enterprise actually creates, captures, transfers, and erodes business value — in practice, not in narrative. This stage answers: Where value is created, How value flows through the enterprise, Where value is constrained, leaked, or fragile, Which parts of the business matter disproportionately to outcomes.

**Discovery Domains:**
  - True revenue engines (mechanisms, not labels or reported segments)
  - Value exchange with customers (what customers actually pay for)
  - Revenue mechanics and pricing logic across lines of business
  - Revenue concentration and dependency risks
  - Margin generation vs margin destruction by business line
  - Dependencies and brittleness in revenue generation
  - Structural constraints on revenue engines from Stage 1 findings
  - Value leakage points (cost, churn, inefficiency, constraints)

**Why Business Reality Not Narrative:**
  - Reported segments may not reflect actual value creation mechanisms
  - Revenue recognition may obscure where value is truly generated
  - Customer contracts may reveal different economics than segment reporting
  - Hidden dependencies (vendor partnerships, key customers) create fragilities
  - Stage 1 structural constraints may render certain revenue streams non-portable or at-risk

**Stage 2 Focus:** WHAT IS, not WHAT SHOULD BE. Business model decomposition into value-creating mechanisms. Mapping of revenue, cost, margin, and dependency flows. Identification of where value is processed, engineered, delivered, or lost. Grounding of 'the business' in operational and economic facts.

### Stage 2 Explicit Non Goals


**Prohibited Activities:**
  - Propose strategic options or growth initiatives
  - Recommend investments, divestitures, or transformations
  - Evaluate management quality or execution effectiveness
  - Design target operating models or future-state architectures
  - Assess 'fit' for a specific buyer or investment strategy
  - Develop product roadmaps or innovation agendas
  - Create data or AI strategies
  - Generate recommendations of any kind

**Prohibited Analysis Types:**
  - Strategy assessment (business model discovery ≠ strategy evaluation)
  - Turnaround planning or operational improvement recommendations
  - Competitive positioning evaluation (beyond describing current reality)
  - Investment thesis validation or buyer suitability analysis
  - Performance benchmarking implying 'good' or 'bad' execution

**Boundary Violations:**
  - Using prescriptive phrases: 'should optimize', 'could improve', 'needs to pivot'
  - Characterizing revenue engines as 'attractive' or 'problematic' relative to investment thesis
  - Proposing pricing changes, market expansions, or product strategies
  - Extrapolating from current business model to future potential without evidence
  - Treating discovery findings as implicit recommendations
**Stage 2 Is About:** BUSINESS REALITY, not aspiration. Decomposition, not judgment. Discovery, not prescription.  
**Stage 2 Is Not About:** Strategy, growth plans, turnarounds, roadmaps, recommendations, future-state models.  

### Stage 2 Failure Conditions


**Invalidation Triggers:**


##### Revenue Engines Mirror Reported Segments Without Challenge


**Description:** Analysis accepts management's segment definitions as true revenue engines without validating value creation mechanisms. Treating 'Product Line A' or 'Geographic Region X' as sufficient explanation of how money is made.

##### Value Exchange Lacks Economic Grounding


**Description:** Cannot explain 'where the dollar comes from and why it exists.' Describing what customers buy without explaining pricing logic, revenue triggers, or margin mechanics.

##### Structural Constraints from Stage 1 Ignored


**Description:** Business model analysis proceeds without incorporating entity constraints, jurisdictional lock-ins, contractual limitations, or regulatory restrictions identified in Stage 1. Assumes mobility or flexibility that Stage 1 disproved.

##### 'What Breaks If Touched' Not Explicit


**Description:** Revenue engines described without identifying dependencies, fragilities, or failure modes. No touch test applied to determine what happens if revenue source is disrupted, moved, or changed.

##### Hypotheses Implicit or Untested


**Description:** Business model assertions made without explicit hypothesis development, supporting evidence, disconfirming evidence searches, or falsification attempts.

##### Uncertainty Smoothed or Resolved Prematurely


**Description:** Unknowns treated as knowns. Ranges collapsed to point estimates. Ambiguity resolved by assumption rather than preserved and documented.

##### Claims Lack Traceable Evidence Lineage


**Description:** Revenue figures, customer data, pricing mechanics cited without source attribution. Mixing FACT (from documents) with INFERENCE (derived) with UNKNOWN (not found) without clear tagging.

##### Analysis Drifts Into Strategy or Prescription


**Description:** Discovery findings presented with implicit or explicit 'shoulds.' Business model analysis becomes business model assessment with judgment about quality or improvement opportunities.

**False Completeness Indicators:**
  - Revenue engines described but value capture mechanisms unexplained
  - Customer segments listed but value exchange logic missing
  - Pricing models stated but margin economics not decomposed
  - Dependencies mentioned but fragility drivers unspecified
  - Concentration risks noted but impact of loss scenarios not quantified
  - Stage 1 constraints acknowledged but not integrated into revenue portability analysis

**Outputs That Signal Failure:**
  - Conclusions that certain revenue is 'good' or 'bad' without defining criteria
  - Recommendations disguised as observations (e.g., 'the business would benefit from...')
  - Treating reported segment profitability as actual economic value creation without validation
  - Ignoring that revenue may be structurally locked to entities that cannot be moved (per Stage 1)
  - Presenting business model as unified when Stage 1 proved jurisdictional fragmentation prevents consolidation

### Dependencies From Stage 1


**Binding Constraints:**
  - Entity structure and jurisdictional separation must be respected in revenue attribution
  - Government/sovereign revenue tied to specific entities with non-transferable authorizations
  - Multi-jurisdictional operations preventing consolidation must inform business model fragmentation analysis
  - Debt covenants and change-of-control provisions affect revenue portability and buyer constraints
  - Contractual obligations (customer, vendor, partner agreements) constrain business model flexibility

**What Breaks If Stage 1 Ignored:**
  - Revenue attributed to 'the company' when actually locked in separate jurisdictional entities
  - Business model appears unified when Stage 1 proved it's portfolio of separate businesses
  - Value transfer assumes flexibility when Stage 1 identified permanent structural lock-ins
  - Integration synergies assumed when Stage 1 proved consolidation is legally/operationally prohibited

**Stage 1 Outputs Required For Stage 2:**
  - Entity structure map (which revenue flows through which legal entities)
  - Jurisdictional constraints (FedRAMP government entity, UK Sovereign isolation, China local entity)
  - Contractual restrictions (debt covenants, customer change-of-control provisions, vendor agreements)
  - Ownership and control reality (Apollo 53-57% ownership, public shareholders, board control)
  - Historical path dependencies (acquisitions, LBO legacy, IPO structure)

### Stage 2 Hypothesis Register


**Governance Note:** Per Stage 0.3 requirements: hypotheses must be falsifiable, evidence-based (not narrative-based), focused on business model mechanics (not performance quality)

**Hypothesis Development Approach:**
  - Develop hypotheses about HOW the business makes money, not WHETHER it makes money well
  - Test value creation mechanisms against evidence (contracts, pricing data, customer interviews)
  - Seek disconfirming evidence that challenges assumptions about revenue engines
  - Preserve uncertainty when evidence is insufficient to support or refute hypothesis

**Example Hypotheses For Orientation Only:**

**Example:** Revenue Engine 1 generates value through X mechanism, priced via Y logic, dependent on Z partnerships  

**Falsifiable Via:** Customer contracts showing actual pricing terms, partner agreements showing revenue share or dependencies, financial disclosures showing margin by segment
**Example:** High-margin revenue is concentrated in business line A which is structurally locked to Entity X per Stage 1 constraints  

**Falsifiable Via:** Segment profitability data, entity-level revenue attribution, regulatory authorizations tied to specific entity, portability restrictions
**Example:** Revenue trigger is consumption-based (not subscription), creating volatility and low customer switching costs  
**Falsifiable Via:** Customer contracts showing billing terms, revenue recognition policies, churn rates, contract duration analysis  
**Note:** Actual hypotheses will be developed during sub-stage execution, not in orientation.
