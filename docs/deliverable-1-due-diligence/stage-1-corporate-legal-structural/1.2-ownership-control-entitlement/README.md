### Control Reality

**Actor:** Apollo Global Management, Inc.  
**Type:** SHAREHOLDER  

**Formal Rights:** 53-57% equity ownership provides: (1) Majority voting control on matters requiring simple majority (director elections, bylaw amendments, certain mergers), (2) Two board seats held by Apollo affiliates (Aaron Sobel - Principal, Apollo; Vikram Mahidhar - Operating Partner, Apollo), (3) Controlled Chairman position until January 2025 (David Sambur, Apollo Partner), (4) Likely registration rights, information rights, and board nomination rights per typical investor rights agreement (not publicly disclosed but standard for PE-backed public companies).

**Informal Or Effective Control:** Apollo exercises EFFECTIVE CONTROL beyond formal ownership percentage through: (1) Influence over board agenda and strategic direction via affiliated directors, (2) Access to non-public financial and operational information through board participation, (3) Ability to approve or reject CEO and CFO appointments, (4) Influence over executive compensation and incentive structures, (5) Reputation effects - management knows Apollo can remove them via majority vote, creating deference to Apollo preferences. Market cap of ~$120M on 53-57% stake means Apollo's investment is ~$64-68M - modest exposure may create misalignment with total enterprise value preservation.

**What They Can Block:** Apollo can BLOCK via majority vote: (1) Any board election outcome not approved by Apollo, (2) Bylaws amendments, (3) Certain merger, consolidation, or asset sale transactions requiring shareholder approval (typically >50% of assets), (4) Amendments to certificate of incorporation, (5) Dissolution of company. Apollo can FORCE via majority vote: (1) Going-private transaction (subject to fairness opinion and appraisal rights), (2) Sale of company if buyer acceptable to Apollo, (3) Replacement of independent directors not aligned with Apollo strategy. Apollo can INFLUENCE but not unilaterally decide (requires board majority or supermajority): Major acquisitions, debt restructurings, significant capex programs.

**What They Can Block:** Lenders can BLOCK: (1) Any change of control at Rackspace Technology, Inc. level (triggers mandatory offer or requires consent), (2) Material asset sales or divestitures that would reduce collateral value, (3) Dividend payments or other restricted payments to shareholders, (4) Incurrence of additional debt beyond permitted baskets, (5) Mergers, consolidations, or corporate restructurings affecting borrower or guarantor entities, (6) Creation of liens on assets (other than permitted liens), (7) Investments or acquisitions requiring new debt. Lenders can FORCE: (1) Full debt repayment upon change of control if mandatory offer not accepted, (2) Acceleration and foreclosure on collateral upon event of default, (3) Replacement of management if default persists (via lender-controlled board post-foreclosure).

Public shareholders CANNOT BLOCK: Director elections, simple majority mergers, asset sales not requiring shareholder vote, debt incurrence (creditor decision), operational changes (board/management authority). Public shareholders can FORCE (via litigation): Appraisal proceedings if taken private, derivative claims for breach of fiduciary duty (subject to demand refusal and board independence), class actions for disclosure violations or fraud.

NASDAQ can FORCE: (1) Delisting if non-compliance persists (company can appeal to NASDAQ Hearings Panel), (2) Additional disclosure via comment letters or deficiency notices, (3) Trading halts for material news or regulatory concerns.

## Hypotheses


### Hypothesis Framework


#### H1: Control is concentrated in a single actor (Apollo Global Management) with minimal effective constraints from other stakeholders.


**Supporting Evidence Sought:**
  - Apollo majority ownership (>50%) documented
  - Apollo board control via affiliated directors
  - Absence of supermajority voting requirements limiting Apollo's power
  - Minimal creditor covenants or easy compliance
  - Management fully aligned with Apollo (high equity ownership, Apollo-selected executives)
  - Public shareholders passive with no activist involvement

**Disconfirming Evidence Sought:**
  - Creditor negative covenants limiting Apollo's action without lender consent
  - Supermajority requirements for material actions
  - Independent directors outnumbering Apollo affiliates
  - Shareholder rights plan (poison pill) limiting Apollo's control
  - Management with independent power base or conflicting interests
  - Public shareholder activism or litigation constraining Apollo
**Status:** ⚠️ WEAKENED  

**Notes:** SUPPORTING EVIDENCE: Apollo has 53-57% majority ownership and 2+ board seats, controls Chairman until Jan 2025, can elect entire board via majority vote. DISCONFIRMING EVIDENCE FOUND: (1) First Lien Lenders have VETO POWER via negative covenants over M&A, asset sales, dividends, additional debt - Apollo CANNOT act without lender consent on material transactions. (2) Change-of-control provisions require lender consent or debt repayment - Apollo CANNOT sell company without addressing $1.3B debt. (3) Public shareholders have appraisal rights creating cost/delay for going-private. (4) NASDAQ delisting risk (stock <$1.00) creates external constraint. (5) Management has operational control and can obstruct Apollo directives. CONCLUSION: Control is CONCENTRATED in Apollo for governance decisions (board, management appointments) but CONSTRAINED by creditors for capital structure decisions. Apollo's control is INCOMPLETE - must negotiate with lenders for material transactions. Hypothesis WEAKENED.

---


#### H2: Economic entitlement is misaligned with control, creating principal-agent conflicts.


**Supporting Evidence Sought:**
  - Apollo controlling with <50% economic stake (super-voting shares or disproportionate board control)
  - Public shareholders with economic stake but no governance rights
  - Management with operational control but minimal equity ownership
  - Creditors with veto power but no equity upside
  - Divergent incentives between stakeholder groups

**Disconfirming Evidence Sought:**
  - Apollo ownership percentage equals voting power (one-share-one-vote)
  - All stakeholders hold proportionate equity and control
  - No dual-class share structure
  - Management equity ownership >25% creating strong alignment
  - Creditors hold equity warrants or conversion rights
**Status:** ✅ SUPPORTED  

**Notes:** SUPPORTING EVIDENCE: (1) Apollo 53-57% economic stake but ABSOLUTE governance control (board, management, strategic decisions). (2) Public shareholders 43-47% economic stake but ZERO meaningful control (cannot elect directors, cannot block actions). (3) Management has OPERATIONAL control but likely <5% equity stake (prioritizes employment over shareholder value). (4) Lenders have $1.3B exposure and VETO POWER but NO equity upside (fixed interest, no warrants). (5) Apollo's $64-68M exposure is <6% of enterprise value ($1.3B debt + $120M equity) yet Apollo controls strategy. DISCONFIRMING EVIDENCE SOUGHT BUT NOT FOUND: No evidence of one-share-one-vote alignment. No management equity stakes disclosed suggesting >25% ownership. No lender equity kickers. CONCLUSION: Misalignment is PERVASIVE. Apollo controls with modest economic exposure. Public shareholders have economic stake without control. Creditors have veto power without upside. Management controls operations without significant equity. Hypothesis STRONGLY SUPPORTED.

  - Preferred stock with class voting rights

  - Single class of common stock with no preferred
  - No poison pill disclosed in SEC filings
  - Credit agreement terms fully disclosed with no hidden consent requirements
  - No strategic partnerships with governance rights
  - No regulatory approvals required for ownership changes
**Status:** ❌ REFUTED  

**Notes:** DISCONFIRMING EVIDENCE: (1) Public shareholders are common stock holders with simple majority voting (Apollo controls). No evidence of supermajority requirements beyond typical DGCL provisions (e.g., 66.7% for charter amendments). (2) No dual-class or preferred stock structure identified. (3) No shareholder rights plan (poison pill) disclosed in recent SEC filings or press releases. (4) Credit agreement negative covenants are DISCLOSED and substantial but not 'hidden' - they are publicly described in press release and Form 8-K. (5) No evidence of strategic partner veto rights. (6) H3 in Stage 1.1 found NO structural regulatory licenses requiring government approval for ownership changes. SUPPORTING EVIDENCE (LIMITED): Creditor veto rights exist but are DISCLOSED, not hidden. NASDAQ listing creates constraint but is regulatory, not stakeholder-specific. CONCLUSION: No HIDDEN veto rights identified. Creditor constraints are substantial but KNOWN. Apollo's control is constrained by disclosed creditor covenants, not by hidden minority protections. Hypothesis REFUTED.

## Misalignment Risks


### Misalignment Risks

**Misalignment Type:** ECONOMIC_EXPOSURE_WITHOUT_CONTROL  
**Affected Party:** Public Shareholders (43-47% economic ownership)  

**Description:** Public shareholders hold ~43-47% of economic value (~$52-56M at current $120M market cap) but exercise ZERO meaningful control over corporate actions. Cannot elect directors (Apollo controls board elections via majority vote). Cannot block mergers, asset sales, or going-private transactions requiring simple majority. Cannot force strategic changes or M&A processes. Cannot influence financing decisions (board/creditor authority). Economic entitlement exists without governance participation.

**Risk Manifestation:** Apollo can execute transactions favoring Apollo's interests at expense of public shareholders: (1) Going-private transaction at low valuation (current $0.48 price creates cheap buyout opportunity for Apollo), (2) Asset stripping or dividend recaps benefiting Apollo disproportionately, (3) Related-party transactions with Apollo affiliates at non-arm's length terms, (4) Strategic decisions prioritizing Apollo's exit timing over long-term value creation. Public shareholders' only recourse is appraisal rights (costly, uncertain) or litigation (faces business judgment rule deference). Market price of $0.48 may reflect PUBLIC SHAREHOLDER DISCOUNT for lack of control.

**Decision Impact:** MATERIAL: Any transaction requiring public shareholder participation (e.g., tender offer, merger requiring supermajority) must account for public shareholder distrust and potential appraisal/litigation risk. Acquirer would inherit this misalignment if buying less than 100%. Going-private transaction to eliminate public shareholders requires fairness opinion, special committee review, and appraisal rights - adding cost and time.
**Claim Type:** `INFERENCE`  

**Evidence Sources:**
  - Apollo 53-57% ownership provides majority voting control per DGCL simple majority standards
  - Public ownership 43-47% calculated as residual
  - Stock price $0.48 and market cap $120M per Yahoo Finance February 4, 2026
  - Delaware appraisal rights and fiduciary duty standards well-established in case law

**Affected Party:** Apollo Global Management (53-57% ownership but disproportionate control)
