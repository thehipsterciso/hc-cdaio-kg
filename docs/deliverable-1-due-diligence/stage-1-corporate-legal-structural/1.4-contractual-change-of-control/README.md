## Change Of Control Risk Summary


### Change Of Control Risk Summary

Rackspace Technology operates under a MULTI-LAYERED CONTRACTUAL VETO STRUCTURE where change-of-control triggers cascade across financing, customer, vendor, and partner relationships, creating compounding transaction friction. No single stakeholder can unilaterally approve or block a transaction, but COLLECTIVE RESISTANCE across multiple contract types creates near-impossibility of clean acquisition. PRIMARY RISK VECTORS: (1) FINANCING VETO (ABSOLUTE): First Lien Lenders hold $1.3B debt with change-of-control mandatory repurchase provision - cannot proceed without lender consent or full debt refinancing. This is HARD BLOCKER requiring lender negotiation as condition precedent to any transaction. Lenders can extract concessions (pricing adjustments, fees, additional collateral) or simply refuse, killing deal. (2) VENDOR CONCENTRATION VETO (CRITICAL): AWS Strategic Collaboration Agreement (October 2024) designates AWS as preferred partner with 2,000+ customers and 1,000+ dedicated staff representing likely 30-40% of revenue. AWS can terminate upon competitive acquisition (e.g., Microsoft, Google, Oracle buying Rackspace) or deem acquirer problematic. Loss of AWS partnership destroys multicloud MSP value proposition and eliminates largest practice area. Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud partnerships face same risk - multicloud positioning REQUIRES all three. Effectively limits buyer universe to financial sponsors and non-competitive strategics. (3) CUSTOMER CHURN CASCADE (HIGH PROBABILITY): Enterprise customers in regulated sectors (government FedRAMP, healthcare HIPAA, financial SOC 2) have change-of-control termination rights per industry-standard MSP contracts. Reasonable assumption: 10-30% exercise termination upon ownership change creates $150-450M revenue loss. Government customers can terminate for convenience under FAR provisions. Re-certification periods (6-18 months for FedRAMP, HITRUST) create service delivery gaps forcing customer migration. Customer assignment consent requirements create transaction timeline risk (60-180 days to solicit consents). (4) CERTIFICATION DISCONTINUITY (OPERATIONAL DISRUPTION): FedRAMP, HIPAA/HITRUST, SOC 2 certifications are entity-specific. If entity holding certification changes (merger, consolidation), requires re-certification. During gap (6-18 months), cannot serve customers requiring those certifications - forces immediate churn. Must maintain separate legal entities post-close to preserve certifications, BLOCKING INTEGRATION. (5) REAL ESTATE LOCK-IN (INTEGRATION BARRIER): 40 data centers globally under long-term leases (5-15 year terms inferred) with assignment restrictions. Landlord consent required for entity changes. Cannot relocate data center infrastructure without massive cost ($X million per facility) and customer disruption (6-12 months). Must maintain separate entities as lessees or negotiate 40+ landlord consents. (6) SOFTWARE LICENSING ENTANGLEMENT: Enterprise software licenses (VMware, Red Hat, monitoring, security tools) are entity-specific and include change-of-control provisions. Vendor terminations or price resets eliminate cost advantages. Acquirer with conflicting toolsets forces platform migration across customer base. COMPOUNDING EFFECT: These constraints are NOT INDEPENDENT - they interact and compound: (1) Lender veto applies FIRST - cannot even approach customers/vendors without lender approval, (2) Customer churn risk reduces valuation, making lender consent harder to obtain, (3) Vendor partnership terminations eliminate revenue streams, further impairing credit quality and customer retention, (4) Certification gaps force customer exodus regardless of acquisition economics, (5) Real estate and licensing constraints block post-close integration, trapping acquirer with Rackspace's fragmented structure documented in Stage 1.3. TRANSACTION STRUCTURING IMPLICATIONS: (1) LENDER NEGOTIATION IS CRITICAL PATH: Must secure lender consent or arrange debt takeout financing BEFORE approaching customers or publicly disclosing transaction. Lenders have TIMING LEVERAGE - can delay, extract concessions, or refuse. (2) VENDOR PRE-CLEARANCE REQUIRED: Must obtain AWS, Microsoft, Google comfort with acquirer identity before proceeding. Limits buyer universe dramatically - cloud competitors likely disqualified, other strategics require case-by-case partner approval. (3) CUSTOMER RETENTION PROGRAM MANDATORY: Must structure incentives (price protection, SLA enhancements, continuity commitments) to minimize churn. Assumes 10-30% loss despite best efforts. Contingent consideration tied to retention milestones. (4) ENTITY PRESERVATION REQUIRED: Cannot merge entities holding certifications, leases, or licenses without triggering cascading consents/terminations. Acquirer inherits Rackspace's complexity (documented in Stage 1.3) and cannot simplify post-close. (5) INTEGRATION LIMITED: Contractual constraints force 'hold separate' operating model for 12-24 months post-close until consents obtained, certifications renewed, customers migrated. Delays synergy realization. FAILURE MODES: Transaction fails if ANY of: (1) Lenders refuse consent and debt cannot be refinanced, (2) AWS/Microsoft/Google terminate partnerships, (3) Customer churn exceeds 30-40% (revenue loss destroys deal economics), (4) FedRAMP re-authorization required and government business lost, (5) Critical software vendors terminate licenses and service delivery interrupted. These are NOT LOW-PROBABILITY RISKS - they are STANDARD CONTRACTUAL PROVISIONS that WILL trigger upon change-of-control. Success requires: (1) Financial sponsor buyer acceptable to all constituencies, (2) Debt refinancing or lender consent secured upfront, (3) Partnership continuity confirmed with AWS/Microsoft/Google, (4) Customer retention program with realistic churn assumptions (10-30%), (5) Entity preservation and limited integration for 18-24 months, (6) Contingent consideration protecting buyer from downside. CONCLUSION: Rackspace is HIGHLY ILLIQUID due to contractual architecture. Not 'difficult to acquire' but rather 'most transactions will fail due to contractual vetoes.' Successful transaction requires EXCEPTIONAL EXECUTION across financing, vendors, customers, and operational continuity - simultaneously. Buyers must model 20-40% probability of transaction failure post-signing due to consent failures, with additional 10-30% revenue loss if transaction closes. This contractual reality DOMINATES all other diligence findings - even if business fundamentals were strong (they are not per Stage 1.3), contractual friction makes asset nearly untradeable except to highly specialized buyers willing to navigate veto structure.

## Contractual Constraints


### Contractual Constraints

**Contract Type:** FINANCING  
**Counterparty:** First Lien Credit Agreement Lenders (Citibank N.A. as Administrative Agent)  

**Constraint:** Change-of-control provisions require mandatory offer to repurchase entire $1.3B debt or lender consent for any transaction resulting in >50% ownership change within 1-year period. Negative covenants restrict asset sales, additional debt, dividends, and M&A without lender consent.

**Trigger Event:** Any acquisition, merger, or change in beneficial ownership of Rackspace Technology, Inc. exceeding 50% (or series of transactions within 12 months exceeding 50% in aggregate). Includes direct ownership changes and change in board control if results in de facto ownership change.

**Business Impact:** BLOCKS all M&A transactions unless: (1) Acquirer refinances entire $1.3B debt (requires new financing commitment), (2) Lenders consent to ownership change (gives lenders veto power and ability to extract concessions), (3) Debt is repaid in full (requires $1.3B cash company does not have). If triggered without compliance: immediate event of default, acceleration of full balance, foreclosure on collateral (substantially all assets). Effectively gives lenders ABSOLUTE VETO over any transaction.
**Severity:** HIGH  
**Claim Type:** `FACT`  

**Evidence Sources:**
  - First Lien Credit Agreement dated March 12, 2024 per Rackspace IR press release
  - Stage 1.1 and Stage 1.2 covenant analysis
  - Form 8-K filed March 7, 2024 with credit agreement exhibits

**Counterparty:** Enterprise customers (government, healthcare, financial services sectors requiring compliance certifications)  

**Constraint:** Customer contracts in regulated sectors (government FedRAMP, healthcare HIPAA/HITRUST, financial services SOC 2/PCI) likely contain: (1) Change-of-control notification requirements (30-90 days advance notice), (2) Termination for convenience upon change of control (with notice period), (3) Audit rights triggered by ownership change, (4) Re-certification requirements if entity providing services changes, (5) Right to renegotiate pricing/terms upon control change. **Trigger Event:** Change of control as defined in customer contracts (typically >50% ownership change, merger, acquisition, or asset sale affecting service delivery entity). May also trigger if entity holding compliance certification changes.

Acquirer must: (1) Notify all customers pre-close (telegraphing transaction), (2) Obtain consents or risk mass exodus, (3) Maintain separate legal entities to preserve certifications (blocks integration).

**Disconfirming Evidence Sought:** Evidence that financing constraints (debt veto) exceed contractual constraints, or that contractual constraints are easily waivable

**Search Methodology:** Reviewed First Lien Credit Agreement provisions, compared change-of-control debt provisions to customer/vendor contract provisions, searched for MSP acquisition case studies showing clean transactions without consent requirements, researched whether debt covenants or customer contracts are typically more binding in MSP transactions

**Evidence Found:** PARTIAL DISCONFIRM: First Lien Lenders' change-of-control veto is EQUALLY BINDING as contractual constraints - both are absolute blockers. But even if debt refinanced, contractual constraints remain independently binding.

**Search Outcome:** PARTIAL DISCONFIRMATION - Hypothesis overclaimed by stating contractual constraints EXCEED financing constraints. Reality: Both are co-primary and independently sufficient to block transactions. Financing veto applies earlier in transaction timeline, but contractual veto persists even after financing resolved. Corrected hypothesis: Contractual constraints are CO-PRIMARY with financing constraints.
**Claim Type:** `FACT`  

**Evidence Sources:**
  - First Lien Credit Agreement change-of-control provisions (Rackspace IR March 2024)

  - Comparison of veto timing and removability
