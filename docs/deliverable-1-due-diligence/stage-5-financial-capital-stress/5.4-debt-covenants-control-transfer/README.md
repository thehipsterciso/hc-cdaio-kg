**Debt Instrument:** First Lien Credit Agreement ($1.3B Term Loan) - Financial Covenant Breach (Total Leverage Ratio Exceeds Maximum)  

**Trigger Event:** Total Leverage Ratio (Total Debt / EBITDA) exceeds maximum threshold. TYPICAL STRUCTURE: Maximum 5.5x-6.5x for distressed credits (exact level UNKNOWN). Tested quarterly. BREACH OCCURS WHEN: EBITDA declines faster than debt paydown (revenue decline + margin compression reduces EBITDA), OR one-time charges reduce EBITDA (restructuring, asset impairments), OR debt increases (PIK interest, incremental borrowing). ESTIMATED TIMING: 6-15 months given current trajectory (revenue declining 7% YoY, operating margin -6.4% before D&A, negative operating leverage).

**Who Gains Control:** LENDERS (Citibank N.A. as Administrative Agent representing lender syndicate) gain SUBSTANTIAL CONTROL immediately upon breach declaration. Lenders gain: (1) VETO RIGHTS over material decisions (CapEx above threshold, hiring/firing executives, asset sales, vendor changes), (2) APPROVAL RIGHTS for ordinary course expenditures above specified limits (e.g., $1-5M per transaction), (3) RIGHT TO APPOINT turnaround consultant or Chief Restructuring Officer (CRO) at company expense, (4) RIGHT TO ACCELERATE entire $1.3B debt (demand immediate repayment), (5) RIGHT TO FORECLOSE on collateral ('substantially all assets' of borrower and guarantors), (6) BOARD OBSERVER RIGHTS (attend board meetings, review materials, no vote but influence).

**What Is Constrained:** Management LOSES CONTROL over: (1) CASH DEPLOYMENT: Restricted payments basket reduces to ZERO—cannot move cash between entities, pay dividends to Apollo, make investments, prepay junior debt. All cash frozen except lender-approved ordinary course. (2) STRATEGIC DECISIONS: Cannot pursue M&A, divestitures, major contracts, or restructuring without lender pre-approval. Lenders can BLOCK any transaction. (3) OPERATIONAL DECISIONS: Cannot make material changes to business model, product portfolio, customer segments, or go-to-market without lender consent. (4) CAPITAL STRUCTURE: Cannot raise new debt, issue equity (if dilutive to lenders' recovery), or refinance without lender approval. (5) PERSONNEL: Senior executive hiring/firing requires lender consultation or approval. CRO appointment gives lenders DIRECT OPERATIONAL CONTROL through management proxy.

**Time To Impact:** IMMEDIATE upon breach declaration. Credit agreement typically provides 30-60 day CURE PERIOD for financial covenant breaches (can cure by raising equity, selling assets, or improving EBITDA). If not cured within grace period, event of default becomes permanent. Lenders can exercise remedies immediately after cure period expires OR waive default in exchange for AMENDMENT (fees, higher interest rate, stricter terms, equity warrants, operational covenants). TYPICAL TIMELINE: Breach detected at quarter-end → 15-30 days for covenant calculation and reporting → 30-60 day cure period → Lender negotiation 30-90 days → Amendment or acceleration. TOTAL: 2-5 months from breach to full lender control or acceleration.
**Control Shift Magnitude:** SUBSTANTIAL  

**Claim Type:** INFERENCE (covenant structure typical for term loans, exact thresholds UNKNOWN) and FACT (First Lien Credit Agreement March 2024 with financial maintenance covenants)

**Debt Instrument:** First Lien Credit Agreement ($1.3B Term Loan) - Financial Covenant Breach (Interest Coverage Ratio Falls Below Minimum)  

**Trigger Event:** Interest Coverage Ratio (EBITDA / Cash Interest Expense) falls below minimum threshold. TYPICAL STRUCTURE: Minimum 1.5x-2.5x for distressed credits (exact level UNKNOWN). Tested quarterly. BREACH OCCURS WHEN: EBITDA declines (revenue loss + margin compression) WHILE interest expense remains fixed or increases (cash interest on $1.3B debt estimated $100-150M annually at 8-12%). CRITICAL: Interest coverage deteriorates FASTER than leverage ratio when EBITDA collapses but debt remains constant. MORE LIKELY TO BREACH FIRST than leverage ratio if revenue collapse accelerates. ESTIMATED TIMING: 6-12 months if EBITDA declines 20-30% from customer exodus (faster than leverage ratio breach).

**Who Gains Control:** LENDERS gain SUBSTANTIAL CONTROL (same as leverage ratio breach above). Interest coverage breach is FUNCTIONALLY IDENTICAL to leverage breach in terms of lender remedies and control transfer. Difference is SYMBOLIC: interest coverage breach signals 'cannot service debt' (more severe distress signal) vs leverage breach 'balance sheet overstretched'. Both trigger same lender rights.

**What Is Constrained:** IDENTICAL to leverage ratio breach: Cash deployment frozen, strategic decisions require approval, operational oversight by lenders, potential CRO appointment, acceleration risk. Additionally, interest coverage breach specifically signals INABILITY TO SERVICE DEBT from operations, creating URGENCY for lenders to take control before cash depletes entirely.

**Time To Impact:** IMMEDIATE upon breach, same 30-60 day cure period. Interest coverage breach is HARDER TO CURE than leverage breach because: (1) Cannot cure by debt paydown (reducing debt increases coverage ratio, but requires cash company doesn't have), (2) Can only cure by EBITDA improvement (requires revenue growth or margin expansion, both unlikely given trajectory), (3) Or equity injection from Apollo (expensive, dilutive, signals distress). CURE PROBABILITY: LOW if breach occurs. More likely to result in amendment negotiations or restructuring than cure. TIMELINE: 2-5 months from breach to lender control.
**Control Shift Magnitude:** SUBSTANTIAL  

**Claim Type:** INFERENCE (covenant structure typical, exact threshold UNKNOWN) and FACT (First Lien Credit Agreement with financial covenants)

**Evidence Sources:**
  - Same as leverage ratio breach above
  - Stage 5.3: Cash interest estimated $100-150M annually (8-12% on $1.3B debt), EBITDA declining
  - Interest coverage more sensitive to EBITDA decline than leverage ratio—likely to breach FIRST if revenue collapse accelerates

**Debt Instrument:** First Lien Credit Agreement ($1.3B Term Loan) - Negative Covenant Violation (Restricted Payments Exceeded)  

**Trigger Event:** Company makes restricted payment (dividend, investment, intercompany loan, asset purchase from affiliate) that: (1) Exceeds permitted basket (typically $25-75M annually for company this size), OR (2) Causes pro forma covenant breach (Total Leverage Ratio > maximum or Interest Coverage Ratio < minimum after payment), OR (3) Made while event of default exists. OCCURS IF: Management attempts dividend to Apollo, makes acquisition, invests in non-guarantor subsidiary, or moves cash between entities beyond permitted amounts. UNLIKELY to breach unintentionally given current cash constraints—more likely intentional if Apollo attempts to extract value or company makes strategic investment.

**Who Gains Control:** LENDERS gain RIGHT TO DECLARE DEFAULT and ACCELERATE debt. Restricted payments violation is EVENT OF DEFAULT (unlike financial covenant which has cure period, negative covenant breach is immediate default unless waived). Lenders can: (1) Demand IMMEDIATE REPAYMENT of $1.3B, (2) FORECLOSE on collateral, (3) Or negotiate WAIVER in exchange for: amendment fee (1-2% = $13-26M), consent fee, higher interest rate, equity kicker (warrants), stricter covenants. Lenders gain TOTAL VETO over cash deployment—every material cash movement requires advance approval.

**What Is Constrained:** CASH MOVEMENT FROZEN. Management cannot: (1) Pay dividends to Apollo (equity investors locked in), (2) Make acquisitions or investments (strategic flexibility eliminated), (3) Move cash between subsidiaries for strategic purposes (multi-entity structure locks cash in place), (4) Fund non-guarantor subsidiaries (e.g., UK Sovereign expansion, China operations). IMPLICATION: Apollo cannot extract cash return, company cannot pursue growth opportunities, multi-entity operations become rigid and inefficient.

**Time To Impact:** IMMEDIATE upon violation. No cure period for negative covenant breach (unlike financial covenants). Lenders can declare default within DAYS of violation if detected. HOWEVER: Lenders typically NEGOTIATE rather than accelerate (acceleration forces bankruptcy, low recovery). Timeline: Violation detected → Lender notice within 5-15 days → Negotiation for waiver 30-90 days → Waiver granted with fees and stricter terms. TOTAL: 1-3 months from violation to waiver/control tightening.

**Control Shift Magnitude:** PARTIAL (violation itself constrains cash but doesn't transfer operational control) → SUBSTANTIAL (if lenders use violation to extract amendment with operational covenants, CRO appointment, or board seats)

**Claim Type:** INFERENCE (typical restricted payments covenant structure) and FACT (credit agreement contains negative covenants restricting payments)

**Evidence Sources:**
  - Stage 5.3: Restricted payments covenant limits dividends, investments, intercompany transfers
  - Stage 5.3: 'If company in covenant stress, restricted payments basket is ZERO'
  - Industry standard: Negative covenant breaches are immediate events of default (no cure period), S&P LCD covenant analysis
  - Waiver precedents: Lenders extract 1-3% fees, rate increases, equity kickers for covenant waivers in distressed situations

**Debt Instrument:** First Lien Credit Agreement ($1.3B Term Loan) - Material Adverse Effect (MAE) Clause Trigger  

**Trigger Event:** Material Adverse Effect occurs, defined as (TYPICAL): 'material adverse change in business, operations, assets, liabilities, financial condition, or prospects.' VAGUE DEFINITION creates LENDER DISCRETION. Potential MAE triggers: (1) CUSTOMER EXODUS: 15-30% of revenue base terminates (Government customers exit, regulated customers leave due to going concern), (2) HYPERSCALER PARTNERSHIP TERMINATION: AWS, Microsoft, or Google terminates partnership (eliminates ability to serve customers on those platforms), (3) MAJOR CONTRACT LOSS: Largest customer(s) representing >10% revenue terminate, (4) LITIGATION JUDGMENT: Material adverse judgment >$25-50M that threatens operations, (5) KEY PERSONNEL EXODUS: CEO, CFO, CTO, or multiple senior executives depart simultaneously, (6) GOING CONCERN QUALIFICATION: Auditors issue going concern opinion in 10-K, (7) REGULATORY ACTION: FedRAMP suspension, compliance certification loss, government contract debarment.

**Who Gains Control:** LENDERS gain RIGHT TO DECLARE DEFAULT immediately upon MAE determination. MAE clause gives lenders SUBJECTIVE JUDGMENT over what constitutes 'material adverse.' Creates NEGOTIATION LEVERAGE: Lenders can threaten MAE declaration to extract concessions (amendment fees, higher rates, operational covenants, equity warrants) even if MAE borderline. If MAE declared, lenders can: ACCELERATE debt, FORECLOSE on collateral, or DEMAND CURE/REMEDY (asset sales, equity injection, management changes). MAE is LENDER's NUCLEAR OPTION—rarely invoked but creates THREAT that disciplines management behavior.

**What Is Constrained:** MANAGEMENT STRATEGIC FLEXIBILITY constrained by MAE threat. Cannot take actions that risk triggering MAE: (1) Cannot allow major customer losses without immediate replacement (must maintain revenue stability), (2) Cannot allow partnership terminations (AWS/Microsoft/Google relationships become EXISTENTIAL), (3) Cannot tolerate executive departures (must retain key personnel at any cost), (4) Cannot accept compliance failures (FedRAMP, HIPAA suspensions are MAE triggers). MAE clause creates INVISIBLE GOVERNANCE—management constrained by fear of lender reaction even without explicit covenant breach.

**Time To Impact:** VARIES. MAE can be declared IMMEDIATELY upon triggering event (e.g., major customer termination announced), OR may require 30-90 days for lenders to assess impact and declare MAE. Unlike financial covenants (tested quarterly on fixed schedule), MAE is EVENT-DRIVEN and CONTINUOUS—can occur any day if triggering event occurs. UNCERTAINTY: Whether specific event constitutes MAE is subject to LENDER INTERPRETATION and potential LITIGATION (borrower may dispute MAE declaration). Timeline if declared: MAE notice within 5-30 days of event → Negotiation or litigation 30-180 days → Resolution (waiver, amendment, acceleration, or court ruling).
**Control Shift Magnitude:** SUBSTANTIAL (if declared and sustained) or PARTIAL (if used as negotiation leverage without formal declaration)  

**Claim Type:** INFERENCE (MAE clause typical in credit agreements, specific triggers and definition UNKNOWN) and FACT (credit agreements contain MAE provisions)

**Evidence Sources:**
  - Stage 5.3: 'Material customer contract breach or termination may trigger MAE clause allowing lender intervention'
  - Industry standard: MAE clauses in credit agreements give lenders broad discretion to declare default for business deterioration (S&P LCD, Debevoise & Plimpton covenant guidance)
  - MAE litigation: Courts generally interpret MAE narrowly, requiring SUBSTANTIAL LONG-TERM IMPAIRMENT, but definition is fact-specific and creates uncertainty
  - Stage 5.1-5.2: Multiple MAE risk factors present (customer churn risk, partner termination risk, executive attrition, going concern risk)

**Debt Instrument:** First Lien Credit Agreement ($1.3B Term Loan) - Cross-Default to Other Material Debt or Obligations  

**Trigger Event:** Default under OTHER debt instrument or material obligation >$25-75M (typical threshold). Triggers: (1) DEFAULT ON OTHER DEBT: Borrower defaults on any other debt obligation (even if immaterial amount, if material impact), (2) JUDGMENT OR SETTLEMENT: Court judgment or settlement obligation >threshold that is not paid or bonded within 30 days, (3) MATERIAL CONTRACT DEFAULT: Default under AWS/Microsoft/Google partnership agreements (if deemed material obligations), data center lease defaults (if aggregate amount exceeds threshold), major customer contract defaults (if create payment obligations >threshold). CREATES CASCADE: One default triggers term loan default, term loan default may trigger OTHER contract defaults, creating DEATH SPIRAL.

**Who Gains Control:** LENDERS gain ACCELERATION RIGHTS upon cross-default. Even if primary debt (term loan) is performing, default elsewhere gives term loan lenders right to declare default and accelerate. Creates AMPLIFICATION EFFECT: Minor failure in one area (e.g., $30M litigation judgment) triggers $1.3B debt acceleration. Lenders use cross-default as LEVERAGE to extract concessions or force comprehensive restructuring. Who gains control: TERM LOAN LENDERS become SENIOR CREDITORS in any restructuring (secured by 'substantially all assets'), other creditors subordinated or unsecured.

**What Is Constrained:** ENTIRE CAPITAL STRUCTURE becomes UNSTABLE. Management cannot: (1) Allow ANY material default anywhere in organization (vendor payments, leases, judgments, other contracts must be paid timely), (2) Negotiate selective defaults or prioritize one creditor over another (cross-default forces comprehensive approach), (3) Pursue OUT-OF-COURT restructuring of single obligation (restructuring must address term loan or cross-default triggers). IMPLICATION: Company must maintain PERFECT PAYMENT PERFORMANCE on all material obligations or face comprehensive default. Creates RIGIDITY—cannot selectively default even if economically rational.

**Time To Impact:** IMMEDIATE upon triggering event, but NOTICE PERIOD typically 15-30 days for term loan lenders to declare cross-default. Sequence: Material default occurs elsewhere → Borrower must notify term loan lenders within 5-10 days → Term loan lenders have 15-30 days to assess and declare cross-default → Negotiation or acceleration. TOTAL: 1-2 months from triggering event to term loan default declaration. HOWEVER: Many cross-defaults are CURABLE if underlying default is cured (e.g., judgment paid, other debt cured) before term loan default declared.
**Control Shift Magnitude:** SUBSTANTIAL (if cross-default declared and sustained) - forces comprehensive restructuring vs selective approach  

**Claim Type:** INFERENCE (cross-default provisions typical in credit agreements, specific threshold UNKNOWN likely $25-75M) and FACT (credit agreements contain cross-default provisions)

**Evidence Sources:**
  - Stage 5.3: 'Credit agreement contains CROSS-DEFAULT provisions: default under other material debt (>$X million threshold, typically $25-75M) triggers default under term loan'
  - Industry standard: Cross-default clauses in term loans create interdependencies (S&P LCD covenant analysis)
  - Stage 5.3: 'Material customer contract breach or termination may trigger Material Adverse Effect (MAE) clause' (could constitute material obligation default)
  - Potential cross-default triggers: AWS/Microsoft/Google partnership defaults, data center lease defaults (40 facilities), major litigation (Alpha Modus patent case)

**Debt Instrument:** First Lien Credit Agreement ($1.3B Term Loan) - Change of Control Event  

**Trigger Event:** Change of control occurs: (1) OWNERSHIP CHANGE: >50% of voting stock acquired by person or group within 12-month period (Apollo currently 53-57%, so third party acquiring >50% triggers), (2) BOARD CONTROL SHIFT: Majority of board no longer consists of directors approved by existing board or elected by existing shareholders, (3) SALE OF SUBSTANTIALLY ALL ASSETS: Sale of assets representing >50% of consolidated assets or revenues. OCCURS IF: Apollo sells its stake (53-57%) to third party, competing PE firm or strategic acquirer purchases company, asset sale includes core business units (e.g., Public Cloud + Private Cloud), board takeover by activist or hostile transaction.

**Who Gains Control:** LENDERS gain ABSOLUTE VETO over transaction. Change of control provision requires: (1) MANDATORY REPAYMENT OFFER: Company must offer to repurchase 100% of term loan at par (100% of $1.3B = $1.3B cash required), OR (2) LENDER CONSENT: Acquirer must obtain lender approval for transaction to proceed without repayment. LENDERS CAN BLOCK ANY TRANSACTION unless: (a) Acquirer has $1.3B cash to repay debt, (b) Acquirer refinances debt with new lenders (requires new financing commitment), (c) Lenders consent (giving lenders ability to extract CONSENT FEES, higher interest rates, equity participation, or other concessions). PRACTICAL EFFECT: LENDERS CONTROL M&A—any sale, merger, or ownership change requires lender approval. Acquirer must negotiate with BOTH seller (Apollo/management) AND lenders (two-party negotiation creates complexity and cost).

**What Is Constrained:** APOLLO'S EXIT BLOCKED without lender cooperation. Apollo cannot: (1) Sell its 53-57% stake to another PE firm or strategic buyer without triggering change of control, (2) Distribute Rackspace shares to its fund LPs without triggering change of control (if distribution results in >50% aggregate ownership change), (3) Pursue IPO with Apollo selling down below control level without addressing debt. COMPANY CANNOT: (1) Pursue strategic sale to competing MSP, cloud provider, or PE sponsor without lender consent, (2) Sell core business segments (if aggregates to >50% of assets), (3) Accept acquisition offer even if price attractive unless acquirer willing to refinance or lenders approve. IMPLICATION: Lenders have ABSOLUTE VETO over all transactions—can demand FULL REPAYMENT ($1.3B) or extract concessions for consent.

**Time To Impact:** IMMEDIATE upon transaction announcement or ownership change. Change of control is SELF-EXECUTING—no discretion or judgment required (unlike MAE). Timeline: Transaction announced or ownership change occurs → Automatic event of default within DAYS → Company must offer mandatory repayment within 30-60 days (per agreement terms) → If not repaid, lenders can ACCELERATE remaining balance and FORECLOSE. NO CURE PERIOD for change of control—must be addressed upfront in transaction structuring.
**Control Shift Magnitude:** TOTAL - Lenders have absolute veto over any transaction that would change control or ownership structure  
**Claim Type:** FACT - Change of control provision documented in Stage 1.4 and Stage 5.3  

**Evidence Sources:**
  - Stage 1.4: 'Change-of-control provisions require mandatory offer to repurchase entire $1.3B debt or lender consent for any transaction resulting in >50% ownership change'
  - Stage 1.4: 'BLOCKS all M&A transactions unless: (1) Acquirer refinances entire $1.3B debt, (2) Lenders consent (gives lenders veto power), (3) Debt repaid in full'
  - Stage 1.1: Apollo ownership 53-57%—sale of Apollo's stake triggers change of control
  - Form 8-K March 7, 2024: Credit agreement with change of control provisions (per Stage 1.4 reference)

### Control Transfer Timeline Summary


**Immediate Transfer:** Change of control (T+0 days: automatic upon transaction), Cross-default declaration (T+15-30 days: after underlying default), Negative covenant breach (T+5-15 days: immediate default, lender notice within days)

**Near Term Transfer:** Financial covenant breach (T+90-180 days: breach detected quarterly + 30-60 day cure period + negotiation), MAE declaration (T+30-180 days: event occurs, lenders assess, declare or negotiate)

**Control Gradient:** PARTIAL (negative covenant violations constrain cash but not operations) → SUBSTANTIAL (financial covenant breach or MAE creates lender oversight, approval rights, potential CRO) → TOTAL (change of control gives absolute veto, acceleration forces sale/bankruptcy). Control transfer is GRADUATED—each trigger adds lender authority, multiple triggers compound to comprehensive lender control.

**Point Of No Return:** Once financial covenant breach CURE PERIOD EXPIRES (60-90 days post-breach), lenders have permanent default. At this point: (1) Cannot cure without lender waiver (lenders extract fees/concessions), (2) Lenders can accelerate $1.3B at any time (sword of Damocles over management), (3) Company operates under LENDER SUFFERANCE—every material decision requires implicit or explicit lender approval. EFFECTIVE CONTROL has transferred even if lenders haven't formally accelerated—management becomes AGENT of lenders, not shareholders.
