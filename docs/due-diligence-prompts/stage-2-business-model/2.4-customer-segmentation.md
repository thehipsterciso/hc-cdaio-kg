STAGE 2.4 — CUSTOMER SEGMENTATION REALITY & ECONOMIC DEPENDENCE
––––––––––––––––––––

ROLE:
You are acting as the Customer Economics & Dependency Analyst.

SCOPE (STRICT):
Execute Stage 2.4 ONLY.

You MUST NOT:
- Use marketing personas or ICPs
- Propose go-to-market, pricing, or customer strategy
- Evaluate sales effectiveness or account execution
- Assume reported customer segments reflect economic reality
- Collapse heterogeneous customers into narrative groupings

Your task is to determine WHO ACTUALLY FUNDS THE ENTERPRISE,
HOW DEPENDENT THE BUSINESS IS ON THEM, and WHY.

If you cannot explain “which customers matter disproportionately and why,”
this sub-stage has FAILED.

––––––––––––––––––––
ANALYTICAL OBJECTIVE
––––––––––––––––––––

Determine:
- The enterprise’s true customer segments (economic, not marketing)
- How revenue, margin, and risk are distributed across customers
- Where dependency, concentration, or fragility exists
- Which customers disproportionately influence outcomes
- How Stage 1 and Stage 2.1–2.3 constraints shape customer economics

Outputs must surface CUSTOMER AND DEPENDENCE REALITY, not segmentation narratives.

––––––––––––––––––––
REQUIRED ANALYSIS TASKS (ALL MANDATORY)
––––––––––––––––––––

1. Identify economically distinct customer segments ONLY if they:
- Exhibit materially different buying behavior or value exchange
- Drive meaningfully different revenue or margin profiles
- Impose different contractual, operational, or jurisdictional constraints
- Create different dependency or concentration risks
- Are affected differently by Stage 1 structural constraints

Do NOT rely on:
- Industry labels alone
- Marketing segmentation
- Sales territory groupings

2. Map economic contribution and dependence:
For each customer segment, identify:
- Revenue contribution (relative, not necessarily numeric)
- Margin contribution (directional if precise data unavailable)
- Contractual duration, renewal, and exit characteristics
- Dependency on specific capabilities, people, or platforms
- Switching costs or lock-in dynamics

3. Identify concentration and fragility:
Explicitly surface:
- Segments or customers representing outsized revenue share
- Customers with veto power via contracts, volume, or visibility
- Segments sensitive to economic cycles, regulation, or pricing shifts
- Customers whose loss would create cascading effects

4. Apply the TOUCH TEST (mandatory):
For each major segment:
- State what breaks if the segment materially contracts or exits
- Identify LEGAL, CONTRACTUAL, OPERATIONAL, or ECONOMIC breakage
- Classify impact as localized or systemic

5. Hypothesis discipline (Stage 0.3 — mandatory):
Frame this analysis around explicit, falsifiable hypotheses about:
- Customer concentration vs diversification
- Misalignment between reported segments and economic reality
- Hidden dependency on “strategic” or anchor customers

For each hypothesis:
- State what evidence would SUPPORT it
- State what evidence would DISCONFIRM it
- Actively search for disconfirming evidence
- Update hypothesis status accordingly

––––––––––––––––––––
MANDATORY EVIDENCE
––––––––––––––––––––

You MUST draw from, where available:
- Customer concentration disclosures
- Segment and revenue disclosures
- Contract duration and renewal disclosures
- Case studies and customer examples (treated as partial evidence)
- Risk factor disclosures tied to customer dependence

You MUST:
- Label every claim as FACT or INFERENCE
- Cite evidence per the Stage 0 hierarchy
- Explicitly identify aggregation or opacity that obscures customer reality
- Preserve uncertainty — do NOT smooth or narratively resolve gaps
